Foundations

What is a dynamical system?

A system whose state evolves over time according to a rule.

A dynamical system has two essential ingredients. The first is a state: a set of variables that captures everything needed to describe the system at a particular moment. The second is a rule of evolution: a mathematical rule that determines how that state changes over time. Together, these two pieces define the system's behavior.

In computational neuroscience, the state can take many forms depending on the level of description. For a single neuron, it might be the membrane potential and perhaps additional variables such as adaptation or channel gating. For a neural population, it might be the average firing rate, the level of synaptic current, or a small set of latent variables summarizing population activity. The rule governing the evolution of that state is usually written as a differential equation, which expresses the rate of change of each variable as a function of the current state and any external input.

What makes the dynamical systems perspective so useful is that it shifts our focus away from isolated snapshots of neural activity and toward how activity unfolds in time. Instead of asking only what the state is right now, we ask: does the system settle to a stable state? Does it oscillate? Does it amplify small perturbations or suppress them? Does it move toward one of several possible outcomes depending on where it starts? These are the kinds of questions that matter for understanding memory, decision-making, motor control, and many other neural computations.

This perspective is powerful because neural computation is inherently temporal. Brains do not simply represent information statically; they transform, integrate, maintain, and update information over time. Dynamical systems theory gives us a language for describing those transformations. It lets us think of neural activity not just as values changing from moment to moment, but as trajectories moving through a state space, shaped by the underlying structure of the system. That shift, from snapshot to trajectory, is one of the most important conceptual moves in computational neuroscience.

Foundations

State and state space

The state is where the system is. State space is all the places it could be.

The state of a system is a complete description of its condition at a given moment. The state space is the set of all possible states the system can occupy.

For a single neuron with membrane voltage $V$ and a recovery variable $w$, the state is the pair $(V, w)$. For a population model with $N$ firing rates, the state is a point in an $N$-dimensional space. As the system evolves over time, that point moves through state space, tracing out a path called a trajectory.

This geometric viewpoint is central to computational neuroscience. Rather than focusing only on the activity of individual neurons, we can ask where the system as a whole lies in state space, how it moves, and what kinds of regions or structures organize that movement. Ideas like attractors, oscillations, and bifurcations are most naturally understood in these terms.

Equations

Reading a differential equation

The equation tells you the rate of change. The solution tells you the trajectory.

Most models in computational neuroscience use equations of the form:

General form
$$\tau \frac{dx}{dt} = -x + f(\text{input})$$

The left side is the rate of change of $x$, scaled by a time constant $\tau$. The right side says what drives that change. The term $-x$ is a leak: it pulls $x$ back toward zero. The term $f(\text{input})$ is the drive from external inputs or from other neurons.

$\tau$ sets the timescale. A large $\tau$ means the system changes slowly; a small $\tau$ means it responds quickly. In a neuron, $\tau$ is the membrane time constant, typically between 10 and 30 milliseconds.

Reading this equation, you can immediately see that if the input is zero, $x$ decays toward zero over a timescale $\tau$. If the input is strong enough, $x$ rises to a new steady state where $dx/dt = 0$.

Fixed Points

Fixed points and steady states

A fixed point is where the dynamics stop. It is where dx/dt = 0.

A fixed point is a state of a dynamical system that does not change over time. If the system is exactly at that state, it will remain there unless something perturbs it. In mathematical terms, for a system written as $\dot{x} = f(x)$, a fixed point $x^*$ satisfies

Fixed point condition
$$f(x^*) = 0$$

This means that at that state, the rate of change is zero.

It is helpful to think of a fixed point as a place where all of the forces acting on the system balance out. Nothing is pushing the state to move in one direction or another, so the system stays put. In one-dimensional systems, fixed points are the values of $x$ where the curve $f(x)$ crosses zero. In higher-dimensional systems, fixed points are the points where all components of change are simultaneously zero.

In neuroscience, fixed points often correspond to meaningful neural states. A neuron sitting quietly at its resting membrane potential can be thought of as being at a fixed point. A recurrent network that continues firing after a brief input may be sitting at a different fixed point corresponding to persistent activity. A decision-making circuit that has settled into one choice rather than another can also be described as having reached a fixed point. In this way, fixed points are often interpreted as the stable states of neural computation.

A system can have more than one fixed point. When that happens, the system's eventual behavior may depend on its initial condition — where it starts in state space. If the system begins near one fixed point, it may move toward that one; if it begins elsewhere, it may end up at another. This is why fixed points are so important: they tell us what kinds of long-term states the system can support, and how different starting conditions can lead to different outcomes.

One important subtlety is that not all fixed points are the same. Some are stable, meaning that if you perturb the system slightly, it returns to the fixed point. Others are unstable, meaning that even a small perturbation causes the system to move away. This distinction matters a great deal in neuroscience, because stable fixed points are the ones that can robustly represent things like resting states, memories, or decisions, while unstable fixed points often act more like boundaries separating different possible outcomes.

So when we study fixed points, we are really asking two related questions: where can the system stop, and which of those stopping points are actually robust? Those questions are central to understanding how neural systems store information, maintain activity, and transition between different computational states.

Stability

Stability: does the system return?

A stable fixed point pulls nearby trajectories back in. An unstable one pushes them away.

A fixed point is a state where the system stops changing. But once we find a fixed point, the next question is just as important: what happens if the system is pushed slightly away from it? The answer tells us whether the fixed point is stable or unstable.

A stable fixed point is one that pulls nearby trajectories back toward it. If the system is perturbed a little, the dynamics act to restore it, so over time the state returns. An unstable fixed point does the opposite: if the system is nudged even slightly away, the dynamics amplify that deviation and the state moves farther away instead of coming back. So stability is really about how the system responds to small perturbations.

For a one-dimensional system $\dot{x} = f(x)$, this idea has a simple geometric interpretation. A fixed point $x^*$ is stable when the slope of $f(x)$ at that point is negative, $f'(x^*) < 0$. In that case, a small perturbation to the right makes $\dot{x}$ negative, which pushes the state back left, and a small perturbation to the left makes $\dot{x}$ positive, which pushes the state back right. In both cases, the dynamics restore the system toward the fixed point. By contrast, if $f'(x^*) > 0$, then perturbations are pushed farther away rather than corrected, so the fixed point is unstable.

This distinction is fundamental in neuroscience because neural systems must often operate reliably in the presence of noise. A stable fixed point can support a robust neural state — a resting state, a maintained memory, or a persistent representation after a stimulus is gone. The state remains meaningful because small fluctuations do not destroy it. An unstable fixed point cannot hold the system in place for long, but it can still play an important role. It often acts like a decision boundary between different possible outcomes. In a decision-making network, the unstable state may separate two stable choice states, so that once activity moves slightly to one side, the dynamics carry it strongly toward one decision or the other.

So when we study stability, we are asking whether a fixed point can actually support a meaningful computational state. A fixed point by itself only tells us where change stops. Stability tells us whether that state is robust enough to matter.

1D Dynamics

One-dimensional dynamics

Start simple: one variable, one equation, one axis.

In a one-dimensional dynamical system, the state is described by a single variable, and the dynamics tell us how that variable changes over time.

A simple nonlinear example is a firing-rate model with recurrent feedback:

Rate model
$$\tau \frac{dr}{dt} = -r + \phi(wr + I)$$

where $r$ is the firing rate, $\phi(\cdot)$ is a saturating nonlinearity such as $\tanh$, $I$ is external input, and $w$ controls the strength of recurrent feedback. The leak term $-r$ tends to pull activity back down, while the recurrent and external input terms push it up.

When recurrent feedback is weak, the system typically has a single stable fixed point. When recurrent feedback becomes strong enough, the system can develop multiple fixed points. In many cases, this includes two stable fixed points separated by one unstable fixed point, producing bistability. This means the system can settle into one of two different long-term states depending on its initial condition or input history.

A useful way to visualize one-dimensional dynamics is with a flow diagram. At each value of $r$, we ask whether $dr/dt > 0$ or $dr/dt < 0$. If $dr/dt > 0$, the state moves to the right; if $dr/dt < 0$, it moves to the left. Fixed points are the values of $r$ where $dr/dt = 0$. These are the points where the flow changes sign, and the pattern of arrows around them tells us whether they are stable or unstable.

Phase Plane

Two-dimensional phase plane

When a system has two variables, its state is a point in a plane. The phase plane shows you everything about how that point moves.

Consider a system with two variables, $x$ (a fast variable, like voltage) and $y$ (a slow variable, like adaptation). At every point in the plane, the equations tell you the velocity of the state — how fast $x$ and $y$ are each changing. Drawing these as arrows everywhere gives the vector field.

Two special curves cut through this field. The $\dot{x}=0$ nullcline is where $x$ has no tendency to change — on this curve, all motion is purely vertical. The $\dot{y}=0$ nullcline is where $y$ has no tendency to change — motion there is purely horizontal. A fixed point must satisfy both conditions simultaneously, so fixed points are exactly where the two nullclines cross.

Once you have the nullclines and the vector field, you can reason about trajectories without computing anything. Where vectors point toward a fixed point, the system is being pulled in. Where they point away, the state is being pushed out. The whole qualitative story of the system is visible at a glance.
